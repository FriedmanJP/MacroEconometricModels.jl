# [X-13ARIMA-SEATS](@id x13_page)

**MacroEconometricModels.jl** provides a pure-Julia implementation of the US Census Bureau's X-13ARIMA-SEATS seasonal adjustment program. The implementation ports the complete pipeline --- automatic ARIMA model identification, outlier detection, and two decomposition methods --- with zero external dependencies.

- **X-11**: Iterative moving average decomposition (Census Bureau method) using Henderson trend filters and seasonal moving averages with extreme value replacement
- **SEATS**: Signal Extraction in ARIMA Time Series (Gómez & Maravall 1996) via Wiener-Kolmogorov optimal filtering in the frequency domain
- **Automatic model identification**: TRAMO-style search over ARIMA specifications with AICC-based selection
- **Outlier detection**: Iterative forward-addition of additive outliers, level shifts, and temporary changes
- **Calendar effects**: Trading day and Easter regressors for monthly and quarterly data

All results inherit from `AbstractFilterResult` and support `trend()`, `cycle()`, `seasonal()`, `adjusted()` accessors, `report()` for tabular output, `refs()` for bibliography, and `plot_result()` for interactive D3.js visualization.

The airline passengers series (Box & Jenkins 1976) is the classic benchmark for seasonal adjustment. The upward trend reflects post-war growth in air travel, the seasonal component captures the summer peak in passenger volume, and the growing seasonal amplitude makes this an ideal candidate for the multiplicative decomposition or log transformation.

## X-11 Method

The X-11 method (Shiskin, Young & Musgrave 1967; Findley et al. 1998) decomposes a time series into trend, seasonal, and irregular components through four iterative phases of moving average smoothing. In the multiplicative model:

```math
y_t = T_t \cdot S_t \cdot I_t
```

where:
- ``y_t`` is the observed time series
- ``T_t`` is the trend-cycle component
- ``S_t`` is the seasonal component
- ``I_t`` is the irregular component

The algorithm applies a ``2 \times s`` centered moving average for initial trend estimation, computes seasonal-irregular (SI) ratios, extracts seasonal factors by averaging within each period across years, and refines the estimates using Henderson moving averages with extreme value detection. Four iterative phases progressively improve the decomposition.

!!! note "Technical Note"
    The implementation uses Census-standard 25-term (monthly) and 9-term (quarterly) seasonal moving averages with endpoint corrections from the original Fortran weight tables. Henderson filter length defaults to 13 for monthly and 5 for quarterly data. Extreme values beyond ``\sigma_{\text{upper}}`` standard deviations of the median are replaced before final seasonal factor estimation.

```@example x13
r_x11 = x13_filter(airline; frequency=12, method=:x11)
report(r_x11)
```

The X-11 trend tracks the accelerating growth in airline passengers from 1949 to 1960, while the seasonal component captures the pronounced summer travel peak. The irregular component standard deviation indicates the amplitude of residual noise after removing trend and seasonality --- for the airline series, this is small relative to the seasonal swing, confirming a strong and stable seasonal pattern.

## SEATS Method

The SEATS method (Burman 1980; Maravall & Pierce 1987; Gómez & Maravall 1996) performs model-based seasonal adjustment using Wiener-Kolmogorov optimal signal extraction. Given an estimated ARIMA model for the observed series, SEATS allocates the model spectrum to trend, seasonal, and irregular components in the frequency domain.

```math
S_y(\omega) = S_T(\omega) + S_S(\omega) + S_I(\omega)
```

where:
- ``S_y(\omega)`` is the model spectrum of the observed series
- ``S_T(\omega)`` is the trend spectrum, proportional to ``1 / |\phi_T(e^{-i\omega})|^2``
- ``S_S(\omega)`` is the seasonal spectrum, proportional to ``1 / |\phi_S(e^{-i\omega})|^2``
- ``S_I(\omega)`` is the irregular spectrum (flat, no AR denominator)
- ``\phi_T(B) = (1 - B)^d \cdot \phi(B)`` is the trend AR polynomial
- ``\phi_S(B) = (1 - B^s)^D \cdot \Phi(B^s)`` is the seasonal AR polynomial

The Wiener-Kolmogorov filter for each component is ``H_c(\omega) = S_c(\omega) / S_y(\omega)``. These frequency-domain filters are converted to symmetric time-domain weights via inverse cosine transform and applied to the data with reflection boundary extension.

!!! note "Technical Note"
    The spectrum is evaluated on a 2048-point frequency grid. Time-domain filter weights extend to ``\min(3s, T/2 - 1, 120)`` lags on each side to capture the full seasonal structure. Unlike X-11, SEATS produces minimum mean squared error estimates of each unobserved component.

```@example x13
r_seats = x13_filter(airline; frequency=12, method=:seats)
report(r_seats)
```

The SEATS decomposition produces smoother component estimates than X-11 because the Wiener-Kolmogorov filters are optimal under the assumed ARIMA model. For the airline series, the classic ``(0,1,1)(0,1,1)_{12}`` model --- originally identified by Box and Jenkins --- provides the spectral structure that SEATS uses to separate trend from seasonality. Both methods compute the same automatic ARIMA model; the difference is in how the spectrum is allocated to components.

## Automatic Model Identification

The automatic model identification follows the TRAMO approach (Gómez & Maravall 1996). The algorithm searches over ARIMA``(p,d,q)(P,D,Q)_s`` specifications in two phases:

1. **Differencing selection**: Fit airline-type models ``(0,d,1)(0,D,1)_s`` for ``d \in \{0,1,2\}`` and ``D \in \{0,1\}``; select ``(d^*, D^*)`` by minimum AICC
2. **Order selection**: With ``(d^*, D^*)`` fixed, search over ~22 candidate ``(p,q,P,Q)`` tuples --- a focused set covering the most common seasonal ARIMA specifications, not an exhaustive grid

Estimation uses Conditional Sum of Squares (CSS) with Levenberg-Marquardt optimization. The best model is selected by minimum corrected Akaike Information Criterion (AICC):

```math
\text{AICC} = -2 \ell + 2k + \frac{2k(k+1)}{n_{\text{eff}} - k - 1}
```

where:
- ``\ell`` is the CSS log-likelihood
- ``k`` is the total number of estimated parameters (ARMA + regression)
- ``n_{\text{eff}}`` is the effective number of observations after differencing and filtering

```@example x13
r = x13_filter(airline; frequency=12)
report(r)
```

The automatic search selects a seasonal ARIMA model that balances fit and parsimony via AICC. For the airline passengers series, the identified model typically includes seasonal differencing (``D = 1``) reflecting the strong annual pattern, with low-order MA terms capturing the remaining autocorrelation structure. The innovation variance ``\sigma^2`` and AIC provide diagnostics for model adequacy --- lower AIC indicates better fit relative to model complexity.

## Log Transformation

The program automatically selects between log and level specifications by fitting the airline model ``(0,1,1)(0,1,1)_s`` to both ``y_t`` and ``\log y_t``, comparing AICC values with a Jacobian correction for the change of variables:

```math
\text{AICC}_{\log}^* = \text{AICC}_{\log} + 2 \sum_{t=1}^T \log y_t
```

The log transformation is selected when ``\text{AICC}_{\log}^* + 2 < \text{AICC}_{\text{level}}``. Pass `transform=:log` or `transform=:none` to override automatic selection.

```@example x13
r_auto = x13_filter(airline; frequency=12, transform=:auto)
r_log  = x13_filter(airline; frequency=12, transform=:log)
r_none = x13_filter(airline; frequency=12, transform=:none)

# Compare selected transforms
r_auto.transform, r_log.transform, r_none.transform
```

The automatic selector compares the AICC of the airline model fitted to ``y_t`` versus ``\log y_t`` with a Jacobian correction. For the airline passengers series --- a positive, trending series where seasonal amplitude grows proportionally with the level --- the log specification is the natural choice because it stabilizes the seasonal swing across the range of the data. The `:log` and `:none` overrides are useful when the analyst has prior knowledge about the appropriate scale.

## Complete Example

This example performs seasonal adjustment of the Box-Jenkins airline passengers series using both X-11 and SEATS, and compares the results:

```@example x13
# Seasonal adjustment via X-11
r_x11 = x13_filter(airline; frequency=12, method=:x11)
report(r_x11)
```

```@example x13
# Seasonal adjustment via SEATS
r_seats = x13_filter(airline; frequency=12, method=:seats)
report(r_seats)
```

```@example x13
# Compare SA series correlation
sa_x11  = adjusted(r_x11)
sa_seats = adjusted(r_seats)
round(cor(sa_x11, sa_seats), digits=4)
```

```@example x13
# Seasonal amplitude reduction
orig_std = std(diff(airline))
sa_x11_std = std(diff(sa_x11))
round(100 * (1 - sa_x11_std / orig_std), digits=1)
```

The X-11 and SEATS methods produce highly correlated seasonally adjusted series because they use the same estimated ARIMA model. The difference lies in how each method extracts the components: X-11 uses iterative moving averages while SEATS uses optimal spectral allocation. The variance reduction in first differences shows how much seasonal noise the adjustment removes --- for the airline series with its strong summer peak, the seasonal component accounts for a substantial share of month-to-month variation.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Series too short.** X-13 requires at least 3 full seasonal cycles (36 observations for monthly, 12 for quarterly). Short series produce unreliable seasonal factors because there are too few years to average across when estimating within-period patterns.

2. **Wrong frequency.** Passing `frequency=12` for quarterly data or `frequency=4` for monthly data produces nonsensical decompositions. The frequency must match the actual periodicity of the data.

3. **Non-positive values with log transform.** The log transformation requires all values to be strictly positive. If the series contains zeros or negative values, set `transform=:none` or transform the data before calling `x13_filter`.

4. **Confusing X-11 and SEATS seasonal factors.** For the multiplicative model, X-11 seasonal factors average to 1 within each year, while SEATS seasonal factors represent the spectral allocation to the seasonal frequency band. The two can differ substantially in amplitude, even though the seasonally adjusted series are similar.

5. **Calendar effects without proper start dates.** Trading day and Easter regressors require accurate `start=(year, period)` dates to map observations to calendar days. The default `(1,1)` is a placeholder --- pass the actual start date when using `trading_day=true` or `easter=true`.

6. **Interpreting `cycle()` accessor.** For `X13FilterResult`, `cycle()` returns the irregular component (not the cyclical component in the HP filter sense). This maintains compatibility with the `AbstractFilterResult` interface. Use `seasonal()` and `adjusted()` for the seasonal adjustment-specific components.
